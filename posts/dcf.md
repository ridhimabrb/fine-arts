I'm sure when you hear someone mention DCF valuation, the conversation immediately turns ENTIRELY mathematical. Investment bankers seemed to build entire careers around it, analysts spent hours refining spreadsheets full of assumptions, and finance textbooks somehow managed to turn a fairly intuitive idea into pages of equations. Naturally, I assumed it was one of those concepts I'd only understand after taking a proper finance course. But after spending some time with it, I realized the model isn't difficult because of the mathematics. It's difficult because you're trying to answer an impossible question using imperfect information. If I had to explain it to someone, at its core, DCF is really just four questions asked one after another.



The first question is surprisingly simple: how much cash will this business generate in the future? Not revenue. Not profit. Actual cash that the business can generate after paying its expenses and investing back into itself. If I owned an entire company instead of just one share, this is the money I'd ultimately care about because it's the money that could eventually be paid out to owners or reinvested to create even more value. The challenge, of course, is that nobody knows the future. Analysts study financial statements, industry trends, competition, management decisions and economic conditions to build an educated estimate. The more I thought about it, the more it felt less like solving an equation and more like making a series of carefully reasoned assumptions.



Then comes the second question: when will that cash actually arrive? This turned out to matter far more than I expected. If someone offered me ₹1 lakh today or promised the same ₹1 lakh ten years from now, I'd obviously choose the money today. I could invest it, earn interest, or simply avoid the uncertainty that comes with waiting a decade. Finance has a name for this idea: the time value of money. A rupee today is worth more than a rupee tomorrow. Once I accepted that idea, the word discounted in Discounted Cash Flow suddenly made sense. We aren't reducing the value of future cash arbitrarily, we're translating future money into today's equivalent.



The third question is where things become interesting: what discount rate should we use? This isn't just a mathematical input pulled from thin air. It's a reflection of risk. A stable utility company with predictable earnings deserves a different discount rate than a young technology startup whose future is far less certain. Higher uncertainty means investors demand a higher expected return, and a higher discount rate reduces the present value of future cash flows. That also explains why changes in interest rates can significantly affect company valuations. When interest rates rise, the opportunity cost of investing increases, discount rates often move higher, and future cash becomes worth less in today's terms. Suddenly, valuation isn't just about the company anymore, it's also about the broader economic environment.



The fourth and final question surprised me the most because it carries so much weight: what happens after the forecast period ends? Most DCF models only estimate cash flows for the next five or ten years, but businesses don't simply disappear after that. Analysts therefore estimate something called a terminal value, which attempts to capture everything the company is expected to generate beyond the explicit forecast period. I found it slightly funny that after spending pages building detailed yearly forecasts, a surprisingly large portion of the final valuation often comes from this single assumption. It was a good reminder that models are only as reliable as the assumptions behind them.



What I probably appreciated the most was that DCF doesn't pretend to be an exact science. Two analysts can look at the same company and arrive at two different valuations simply because they made different assumptions about growth, risk, or future cash flows. That doesn't necessarily mean one of them is wrong, it means valuation is as much about reasoning as it is about calculation. Changes my perspective of finance; it's not a world of formulas with just one correct answer. Instead, it feels much more like building a structured argument, where every number tells a story and every assumption needs to be justified.



The CS parallel:
DCF reminded me of building a predictive model. You gather historical data, make assumptions about the future, assign weights to uncertainty, and end up with an estimate rather than a guaranteed answer. The tools are different, but the way of thinking feels surprisingly familiar :))
