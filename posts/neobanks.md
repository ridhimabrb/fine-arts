For a long time, I assumed banks and banking apps were basically the same thing. One looked modern, the other looked outdated, but both ultimately let me transfer money, check my balance, and pay bills. Then I started reading about neobanks, and suddenly everyone was talking about the future of finance. Sleek interfaces, zero paperwork, instant account opening, budgeting tools, spending analytics. It almost sounded like traditional banks were about to become obsolete. But the more I read, the more I noticed something interesting. Almost every new fintech startup begins by trying to replace the bank, yet over time, many of them slowly start adding the exact same services banks have offered for decades. It made me wonder whether they're really disrupting banking, or simply rebuilding it with better design?

The word neobank itself is slightly misleading. Most neobanks don't actually function like traditional banks, at least not in the beginning. They often don't hold customer deposits themselves, issue loans using their own balance sheet, or maintain the massive regulatory infrastructure that banks are required to have. Instead, many operate on top of licensed banks using what's often called BaaS (Banking-as-a-Service). The traditional bank quietly handles the regulated backend while the neobank focuses on what users actually see: the app, the onboarding experience, notifications, budgeting tools, customer support, and everything that makes the product feel effortless. Once I understood that separation, I realized a neobank is as much a software company as it is a financial company.

That also explained why building a banking app isn't the hard part. Building trust is. Behind every "Send Money" button is an enormous amount of infrastructure making sure transactions settle correctly, fraud is detected, identities are verified, regulations are followed, and customer funds remain protected. Traditional banks have spent decades building these systems, and they're largely invisible until something goes wrong. As a user, I rarely think about compliance checks, anti-money laundering systems, payment rails, reconciliation, or audit trails. But those boring-sounding processes are exactly what make digital payments feel almost instantaneous and reliable.

This is probably why so many fintech companies eventually begin adding the same features they once claimed they didn't need. First comes a debit card. Then savings accounts. Then lending. Then investments. Insurance. Credit cards. Business banking. Before long, the product starts looking remarkably similar to the institution it originally wanted to replace. The interface feels fresher and the experience is undeniably better, but underneath, many of the same financial problems still need solving. It reminded me of something I see in software all the time. Replacing an old system is easy on a presentation slide. Replacing decades of infrastructure, regulations, edge cases, and customer trust is an entirely different challenge.

I think this article also changed how I look at innovation. Most people imagine disruption as throwing away the old system and starting from scratch. Increasingly, it seems to mean understanding the old system well enough to improve the parts people actually interact with while quietly relying on years of infrastructure beneath the surface. Maybe that's why fintech feels like such an interesting space to me. It's not just about finance, and it's not just about software. It's about designing systems that people trust without ever having to think about them. 

This is probably where my finance rabbit hole begins. There are so many concepts that get thrown around in conversations that sound intimidating until you pause and ask, "Wait... what does that actually mean?" I don't expect to become an expert overnight, but I do want to understand the ideas behind the jargon instead of just recognizing the words. If nothing else, these posts will be a record of that curiosity and I'm writing them because forcing myself to explain an idea is the quickest way to find out whether I've actually understood it :)
